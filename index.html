Fire Ecology Research Fellow (ORISE funding by USFS)

I developed GIS models to assess the potential impact of wildfires on recreational areas within all four National Forests in Southern California. To understand the potential for fire to displace recreation, I modeled recreation displacement likelihood under two weather scenarios: dry and moderate conditions. The models incorporated spatial data inputs including canopy height, flame length probability, heat per unit area, burn probability, and fire severity to evaluate wildfire intensity and potential impact. This work supports forest management and safety planning efforts and was recognized with a Chief’s Award for its contribution.

I designed and implemented GIS tools using Python, R, and Google Earth Engine to automate the downloading and processing of satellite imagery from GOES, VIIRS, MODIS, Landsat, and Sentinel. These datasets were combined to reconstruct the progression and behavior of historical wildfires at a fine temporal resolution. By integrating this imagery with weather data, I analyzed how fires evolved over time and compared these patterns with fire severity models I developed, enhancing understanding of wildfire growth and historical severity.

Graduate Research Assistant, Cal Poly Humboldt

I conducted spatial and statistical analyses to assess changes in forest phenology using satellite imagery, and examined their relationship to shifts in aboveground live biomass and drought-related tree mortality using Forest Inventory and Analysis (FIA) data. I used Python, R, Oracle VM VirtualBox, Ubuntu, and Linux to generate Landsat angle files and produce cloud-free time series imagery. This multistep process, carried out in ENVI/IDL software, included correcting for solar and sensor angle differences, stacking the imagery, masking water pixels, screening for clouds and shadows, and repairing image artifacts. I calculated spectral indices such as EVI, NDMI, and NDVI, extracted forest phenology metrics, and harmonized the remote sensing data with FIA datasets. I also performed multivariate statistical analyses to explore these ecological relationships.

GIS & Remote Sensing Student Research Assistant

I utilized the LANDIS-II forest landscape model to simulate fire emissions in the Lake Tahoe bioregion. In addition, I used Python and R to georeference and extract data from over 30,000 raster images. Data sources such as gridMET, PRISM, and TERRAclimate were integrated with forest modeling tools to estimate fuel loads and fire severity. GPS and GIS software supported site selection for tree core sampling. Project deliverables included technical reports, presentations, and outreach materials such as PowerPoints, published documents, and spatial maps.

Undergraduate Capstone Project – Watershed Modeling With VELMA2.0

In partnership with Oregon State University and the EPA, I modeled Northern California watersheds using VELMA 2.0 software, becoming the first to successfully model watersheds exceeding 100 square miles. This included the Eel, Trinity, and Noyo Rivers. I also created and maintained a website to share results, developed extensive documentation to support software use, and produced interactive charts and maps to communicate key findings.
